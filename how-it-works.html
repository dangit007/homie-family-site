How it works

A calendar knows what’s on. Homie knows what it takes.

Homie sees every plan as the whole household lives it — who’s going, who’s driving, when you actually leave — and weighs it against everyone’s week before it ever lands on the calendar.

The committed window

A calendar saves the event. Homie plans the getting there.

“Soccer game, 9:00 AM” hides the 8:13 AM scramble to get out the door. Homie expands every plan into its real shape — prep, travel, and an arrival buffer around the event itself — and reasons about that, never the bare timestamp.

Schedule · review · plan

Three steps, then it’s off your mind.

Say the plan, review what Homie understood, and it lands on everyone's phone — here's how one soccer tournament goes from a sideline mention to a sorted Saturday.

1

Say it, don’t type a form.

Walking out of practice, Dad holds the mic: “Maya has a soccer tournament Saturday, 9 to 4, at Lakeshore field.” Natural language in, a structured plan out. Voice is transcribed on the phone, and there’s an offline fallback — no signal doesn’t mean no schedule. You pick the verb; Homie never guesses whether you meant to commit.

See what it understood, before it’s real.

Nothing lands on the family week without a one-screen review — the date spelled out, the people named, the place assumption said aloud.

The live fit check spots the catch a calendar can’t: the tournament runs into Leo’s dentist — one driver, two places. Dad flips Covered by to extra help — grandma — and adds it.
